Midterm Exercise

Mishnah Ketubot 3:1

Men who rape a relative or a menstruating woman are forced to pay the woman a financial penalty.

Talmud Ketubot 31b

The Talmud then notes that Mishnah Makkot 3:1 states that in the same list of scenarios where a man rapes a relative, the man is lashed. However, it is an established principle in Rabbinic law that a person cannot be both lashed and forced to pay a monetary penalty for the same crime.

The Talmud posits this central question:

Why don't these two Mishnahs contradict each other?

Ulla's Answer

Ulla says there is no contradiction because Mishnah Ketubot is referring to an adolescent girl (na'arah) whereas Mishna Makkot is referring to an adult woman (bogeret).

In the first case, because the man must pay a fine to the girl's father, he is not lashed. In the second case, because there is no fine paid to the father, lashes are the appropriate punishment.

Question 1

But even in the case of an adult woman, doesn't the offender have to pay penalties for causing shame and blemish?

No, because Makkot is considering a woman who is an "imbecile".

An imbecile, whether adolescent or mature, has no reputation to lose and her virginity is not worth any monetary amount.

Question 1.A

But even if the adult woman is an imbecile, doesn't the offender have to pay penalties for the pain inflicted?

Makkot is referring to a case where the adult woman is seduced, not raped, so she experiences pleasure instead of pain.

Question 2

The Talmud generalizes Ulla's position: when there is a possibility of both fines and lashes, the offender only pays the fine.

From where does Ulla know that monetary penalties are given precedence over lashes?

  • Answer A

    The rules regarding personal injury.

    The rules regarding personal injury say that given the possibility of both fines and lashes, the offender only pays the fine.

    In personal injury cases the financial penalty is five-fold, so it cannot be extrapolated to the cases of incest in the Mishnah.

  • Answer B

    The rules regarding edim zommemim (scheming witnesses)

    The rules regarding personal injury say that given the possibility of both fines and lashes, the offender only pays the fine.

    In the case of edim zommemim, the scheming witnesses can be punished even if they are not given a warning.

  • Answer C

    A combination of the rules regarding personal injury and edim zommemim.

    In both the case of personal injury and in the case of edim zommemim, given the possibility of both fines and lashes, the offender only pays the fine. Therefore it can be extrapolated that in all scenarios the fine takes precedence over lashes.

    Another commonality between the personal injury and edim zommemim is that they are exceptional cases — the personal injury fine is five-fold and edim zommemim do not need a warning — so you cannot extrapolate anything from them.

  • Answer D

    A gezerah shavah connecting the word "for" (tachas)

    Deuteronomy 22:29 refers to a man raping a virgin having to pay a fine "for he has violated her." Exodus 21:24 says that in cases of assault, the compensation must be "an eye for eye." Because both verses use the Hebrew word "tachas" as the word "for," the anonymous Talmudic editor speculates that Ulla might have concluded that because in the case of assault, monetary fines takes precedence over lashes, that principle also applies in the cases of sexual violation.

Rav Johanan's Answer

Rav Johanan says there is no contradiction because even if both mishnahs are referring to an adolescent girl because Ketubot is referring to a case where the offender received prior warning whereas Makkot is referring to a case where the offender received prior warning.

The Talmud generalizes Rav Johanan's position: when there is a possibility of both fines and lashes, the offender is lashed. (In contrast with Ulla's conclusion.)

Question 1

From where does Johanan know that lashes take precedence over monetary penalties?

Deuteronomy 25:2-3

Deuteronomy says "The judge shall cast him down and lash him...in accordance with his wickedness. Forty [lashes] he shall strike him." Because wickedness is a singular noun and it is followed by a count of lashes, the Talmud infers that lashes take precedence.

In the cases of personal injury and edim zommemim, fines take precedence over lashes.

Question 1.A to resolve rejection in Question 1

Why don't these exceptions raise a contradiction?

In the context of Mishnah Makkot 1:2 in which Rav Meir says that edim zommemim both pay the fine and are lashed and in which the Sages say that "whoever pays is not lashed."

Question 1.A.i

Why do the Sages say that fines take precedence over lashes?

R. Illa said that the Torah makes it clear that for edim zommemim, paying a penalty excludes the schemer from receiving lashes.

Question 1.A.i.a

Where does the Torah say this?

  • Answer A

    Midrashic analysis of Deuteronomy 19

    Deut. 19:19 says, in the context of edim zommemim, "And you shall do to him as he conspired to do to his brother." This is repetitive with Deut. 19:21, "hand for hand." The superfluous verse implies that because money is passed from one hand to another, fines take precedence.

  • Answer B

    Midrashic analysis of Leviticus 24

    Lev. 24:19 says "if a man inflicts a wound in his fellow, as he did so shall it be done to him." Lev 24:20 says "just as he gave a would in person, so shall it be given to him." The second phrase is deemed superfluous and is there to teach that the punishment involves something that is given: money.

Question 2

Why does Rav Johanan not agree with Ulla?

Accepting Ulla would mean annulling the law "the nakedness of your sister you shall not reveal." (Lev. 18:9)

Lev 18 says that in all cases of incest, the man must be lashed for seeing his sister naked. However, Ulla says that an offender is not lashed if he inappropriately sleeps with an adolescent girl. Johanan doesn't like this contradiction.

Rejection of Johanan's answer to Question 2

  • Scenario A

    Rav Johanan would annul the verse about personal injury, "Forty [lashes] he shall strike him. He shall not exceed this..."? (Duet 25:2-3)

    This verse contradicts Johanan's belief that lashes take precedence over payment; in most cases of personal injury, the punishment is monetary.

  • Scenario B

    Rav Johanan would annul the verse about edim zommemim, "And it shall be, if the wicked man is punishable by lashes"? (Duet 25:2)

    This verse contradicts Johanan's belief that lashes take precedence over payment. Edim zommemim can only be punished with lashes if exceptional circumstances where the quid-pro-quo punishment isn't applicable.

Because it is possible to limit the fulfillment of the verse in Scenario A to cases like one where edim zommemim testify that a witness is not a Kohen, so to is it possible to limit the verse in Johanan's answer to Question 2 to just the case where the sister is an adult woman.

Question 3

The Talmud now revisits the discussion of Answer 1 to Question 1.A.i.a, where it suggests that for edim zommemim, payment takes precedence over lashes.

Why do edim zommemim pay a fine but are not lashed?

R. Elazar says because they are not given an official warning.

Question 3.A

How do we know this?

Raba says it's impossible to warn scheming witnesses.

Raba cites three scenarios. If you warn them beforehand, edim zommemim will just say that they forgot. If you warn every person right at the moment before they testify, then nobody will ever testify. Warning them at the end serves no purpose.

  • Abaye's Rejection to Raba's answer to Question 3.A

    Abaye says it's possible to warn them immediately after they testify and give them the option to recant their testimony.

  • R. Aha's Rejection to Raba's answer to Question 3.A

    Rav Aha says to warn them from the beginning and give them "hints" to remind the witnesses throughout the testimony so they can't claim to have forgotten.

Question 3.A.i where Abaye Retracts his Rejection

Abaye asks: Do edim zommemim even need a warning?

Abaye answers no, retracting his previous statement and agreeing with R. Elazar

Even if it is technically possible to warn a witness before they could scheme, it isn't a necessary condition to kill them. Abaye notes that the scheming witnesses do not give the defendant a warning that they plan on killing him, so why would that reciprocate back to the edim zommemim?

R. Sama's Rejection to Abaye's answer to Question 3.A.i
